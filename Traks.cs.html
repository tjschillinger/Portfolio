Traks

2016 - UX/UI Designer

The Introduction

Traks is all about stories so it seems best to tell you the story of how we, my husband and I, created it. We were living in Colorado, visiting everywhere from Denver to Grand Junction. One day we were reminiscing about our adventures when the same conclusion came up again and again- “I don’t remember.” It was the death sentence of an experience and that’s what we set out to solve-the resurrection and preservation of a travel experience.

The Task

We were tasked with designing a web application to aid people with organizing their travel experiences -past, present, and future, allowing them to capture all their experiences and access them from anywhere. This project was important to us because of our passion for traveling and our frustrations with displaying our travel portfolio in a beautiful and approachable way.

The Problem

There is no digital space to hold an entire travel experience -such as, personal ratings, media, maps, and journal entries. Moreover, no way existed to share an entire experience or invite others to collaborate. This leaves people full of memories and plans and no network to express and share them.

The Solution

Traks allows people to trace their travels. In one digital space, users can upload media, record their thoughts, rate their individual experiences, visualize their tracks, and share with friends and family. A user can also look forward by planning a journey, which allows them to define destinations, transportation, companions, and travel dates. Or, if the user isn’t quite ready to plan out a journey, they can create a wishlist, which is also visualized on their map. And finally, once a user wants to publish their travels in a physical book, they can publish it through a site like Blurb.

The Survey

To gain insights from our users and protect against biases, we sent out a survey to discover what was meaningful to potential users. This step was crucial because it helps to keep our ideas grounded in reality and the project oriented toward users.

Survey Intentions

With our survey we wanted to understand:

  1. The value of visual representation through a map
  2. The value of a rating system connected to individual experiences of a location.
  3. What factors determine which locations are worth traveling to.
  4. What aspects of a travel are important to store
  5. The value of publishing travel experiences through a site like Blurb.

Survey Results

  1. 70% of people found a map valuable.
  2. 70% of people found ratings to be valuable.
  3. 88% people said cost was important, 55% distance, 55% physical environment, 55% past experiences.
  4. 100% of people found photos to be valuable for storing, 60% videos, 40% journal entries, 40% ratings.
  5. 20% of people found the Blurb feature valuable.

Survey Inferences

Affirmed- The survey informed us that a map, ratings, and media were worthwhile to the MVP.
Denied- We decided to drop the Blurb idea due to a lack of interest. Also, we had the idea to include voice recordings as a media option, but there was a lack of interest in this feature as well, so it too was dropped from the MVP.
Insights- From the survey, we realized that we needed to include a cost rating in conjunction to experience ratings.

The Competition

Traks has two main components: sociability and archive navigability. Furthermore, our website has no direct competition, but its main components did. With these concepts being the goal we primarily looked at three sites: Facebook, Roadtrippers, and Nomad List.

  1. Facebook, while the industry standard of social media, does little over a lot, giving many social options, but lacking depth in individual experience. For Traks, we wanted everything oriented around the user’s travel experiences.
  2. Roadtrippers does a great job allowing the user to plan their trip and their map feature is great for viewing their route. However, it is one-dementional, allowing users to only plan a trip without carrying forward that information as some sort of travel log once the trip is executed.
  3. Nomad List provides an intuitive way to filter choices and rank locations based on what the user chooses, yet it doesn’t allow a way to connect with friends, see their ratings, and collaborate.

The Brand Identity

Traks was intended from the outset to be a personal, yet social platform for one’s travel experiences. Originally, we thought the name of the site would be “Travel Bug,” due to the explorative nature of the site’s users. However, bugs annoy people, and it doesn’t capture the personal and social spirit of the site. Thus, we landed on “Traks,” because it reminds us of a personal imprint left behind by a traveler, something similar to a fingerprint. Naturally, this led to a footprint as the focal point of the logo. As for the social nature of the site, we went with a stamp border, which also tied in nicely with our digital postcard idea (a special feature of the site).

More Brainstorming

For fonts, we wanted the titles to look strong and permanent, so we chose Bitter due to its strong, angular design. As for the rest of the text, we wanted an easy-to-read font that was as non-distracting as possible. Thus, Poppins was our choice, especially since it was crafted with digital screen in mind.

The color palette needed to be comprised of colors that were active yet calming. Active, because of the explorative spirit of our intended users, and calming because we knew that our users would be inputing a lot of information -and traveling can easily lead to stress. We concluded that oranges and blues would dominate the palette.

The Mockups

Since I was still a relatively new designer there are several things I had to discover the hard way through pouring hours of hard work into making the mockups for Traks. The first thing that I did not adequately consider was the scalability of the mockups. I was working away in Sketch without a thought and it wasn't until the mockups were being prototyped in InVision that I realized how ENORMOUS everything was! I had to rescale over two hundred mockups! Guess who's not making that mistake again?

Original Size

Scaled Version

Expert advice through my process informed me that my work was too bulky and boxy. The expert I spoke with said it was a common mistake for newer designers because it helps us place everything when we think of putting everything in boxes. This can create a very closed design and the goal was to try to open it up more. It was a breath of fresh air removing the boxes throughout the site. It did make a cleaner, freer design and has since contributed to my more minimalist design work.

Reflections

This project helped us to begin practicing designing in the wholistic context of the project. We learned to work together as two very different designers to created a website that looked whole. We also learned more about how all the different steps to designing actually fit together to create the “big picture”. User flows only make sense in the context of what you’ll be using them for.

Something cool we started doing was to create a “purgatory” which was a location to place ideas or designs we weren’t sure about. That allowed us to move forward with new ideas unencumbered because we knew we always had a purgatory with our old ideas or designs we could draw from if things went wrong. That allowed us to put our best foot forward and we rarely needed to look back but atleast we had the peace of mind that we could if need be.
